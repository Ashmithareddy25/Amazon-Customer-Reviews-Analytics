I was so excited that I started reading it as soon as it was delivered to my Kindle, at 12:45AM.  So what if I was going to be exhausted at work the next day?  I needed to know what happens!  Sadly, I ended up mad and disappointed.<br /><br />*****SPOILERS BELOW*****<br /><br />My disappointed stems from the fact that I feel like a lot of things were unresolved, dropped from the previous books, or unexplained.  One of the strengths of the first two books is that not only are the main characters fully realized human beings, but the secondary characters are as well.  And yet in Allegiant, they fade into the background.  Uriah is barely mentioned at all despite having suffered a huge loss at the end of Insurgent.  Peter, who ends up being a fascinating and complex character in Insurgent, also fades into the background and is treated as a one note character.  Christina fades into the background to only around to support Tris and act as a sounding board for dealing with grief.<br /><br />The world of the first two books also fades into the background.  Once the characters go beyond the fence, it is like Veronica Roth forgets about the world she created in Divergent and Insurgent and we only get glimpses of what is going on back there.  And while that might make sense since our main characters are now discovering the world beyond the fence, she doesn't do a good job of describing that world to us.  Like is there a central government in this version of the United States?  How much does the rest of the country know about these special communities?  Is it just in the USA? Are the genetically damage individuals a combination of people in the other communities or are they just those who didn't consent to participate?  I really think it would have been better if Roth had split Allegiant into two books.  Notonly could she have had more time to situate the reader in this new world, but she would have had more time to illustrate how this knowledge affected the characters.  We only get glimpses of what they might be feeling, but for the most part everyone except Four/Tobias just sort of deals and then get excited to ride in an airplane.<br /><br />And now let's talk about Tobias...  Now I love him as character (and not just because he is kind of dreamy in a grumpy way).  I love that in the first two books he is his own character.  He has thoughts, feelings, and a purpose outside of just being Tris' love interest, which is refreshing compared to a lot of dystopian YA literature.  I love that he is flawed and has the potential to turn into something dark and bad but doesn't.  But I feel like for a good chunk of Allegiant, I didn't recognize this version of Tobias.  The Tobias from the other two books wouldn't have just gone off with people he barely knew and try to start a revolution.  A lot of his actions and reactions just don't match up with what we know about him.  Granted in the first two books we are seeing him from Tris' perspective, but still, it just didn't fit to me.  I will say though that I did like seeing his perspective on things and I would have really liked it if Roth could have explored the whole issue of genetics vs environment with him.<br /><br />Another aspect that I did like (and accounts for the two star rating instead of one) is the Tris/Tobias relationship.  Although they are at odds with each for chunks of the book, I love the mature way they handled those situations, with open and honest discussions.  The maturity of their interactions was lovely character growth from Insurgent where they really struggled to communicate with each other.  The progression of their relationship was lovely in general to read and really accounts for the two star rating (otherwise I would have rated it less).  However, if Roth really wanted to be ballsy, she would havewritten them a proper sex scene instead of leaving us with vague imagery.  It would have been more realistic, considering how hot and heavy they seemed to be.<br /><br />But now for what seems to be a main beef with a lot of people, the ending...  So I get what Roth was trying to do and my issue is not that Tris died, it has more to do with how it happened.  The way the whole thing plays out, it reads like more of a rough draft.  It just doesn't make sense from a plot perspective and honestly loses impact to me.  It is meant to seem like it was a selfless act, but it really wasn't.  As others have pointed out, Tris basically risks her life in every book so the fact that she does it here doesn't make it shocking.  It reads like she does it because she doesn't want the guilt of having her brother Caleb losing his life instead of her, which ultimately makes it a selfish act.  Additionally, the only sadness I felt with her death was the impact that it has on Tobias.  Her death didn't end up saving anyone since Tobias had already solved the mass genocide issue (which would have probably stopped the memory erasing) so all it means is that Tobias will likely never be happy.  Not really satisfying for this reader.  Honestly, I think the impact would have been greater if Caleb had actually carried out the original plan and we had seen the aftermath of Tris dealing with that (and it seemed like it was set up for that since Caleb was due some character redemption) or if Tobias had died.  Sure Roth would have pissed off a lot of fans but I think the impact of Tobias dying would have been more poignant as we grieved with Tris and would have made the message of mending together stronger.  Tris is who we know best so it doesn't make sense to kill her off or try to turn her into some christ-like figure.<br /><br />So there you have it, in a long and rambling review.  I wanted to love it.  But I really couldn't.
