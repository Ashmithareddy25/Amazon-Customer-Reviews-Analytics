It read specifically like a fandom smack down - with the author leaving no stone unturned to be sure his fans (those awful \\"literary fangbangers\\") got the message.  Sookie said she couldn't wait for him to leave town, and I have to say that ultimately I agreed with her.  Get outta town before the Maker does anything more horrifying to you, Eric.  Run for your undead life, and never look back.<br /><br />7. The pace of the novel is erratic.  I found the first half a chore to read, and put it down several times - an absolute first for me with any Sookie novel - and though it does pick up towards the end, it sort of slides into an unsatisfying conclusion.<br /><br />8.  I used to really enjoy the little snippets of \\"real\\" life in Sookie's story, seeing them as a pleasant juxtaposition to the outrageous goings on in her forays in the supernatural world.  But this time it felt like a LOT of filler to make the time pass within the story - as if the author needed a few days to pass for what came next to seem plausible, but she just didn't have any story with which to fill that time.  And jeez, was there enough eating in this book?  I have to worry enough about that in my real life, thanks.<br /><br />9. Continuity has never been Ms. Harris' strong suit, even with an editor specifically for this purpose, but little mistakes with names, etc. have never really bothered me.  Adherence to previously established world rules, however, is job number one in any paranormal/fantasy book series.  This book tosses all that out the window in epic fashion.  Despite multiple instances of Sookie being able to read shifter thoughts with her telepathy scattered throughout the novels, suddenly Sookie tells Sam she can only get a general sense of his emotions.  Say what?  Funny how that little impediment to their relationship could just be rewritten, Just. Like. That.<br /><br />10. The sex scene in this book was the most uncomfortable to read of any in all 13 books, and that's really saying something when a lot of those other scenes have involved biting and blood sharing, not to mention \\"quivering puppies\\".  I thought the passion between Sookie and Sam felt forced, and the comparison to \\"seals\\" was a most unfortunate turn of a phrase considering the reader is well aware that Sam can shift into any animal.  Yuck.<br /><br />Positives?  As usual, Ms. Harris has a keen ear for character dialogue.  Bon Temps continues to feel like a real place, and she can certainly describe a unique character in a way that puts the reader right in the scene.  For those reasons alone, I give her one star.  Overall, it was a sad end to a previously enjoyable series - and one that left an awful taste in thisreader's mouth and changed my opinion of the series as a whole.  I had been spoiled (by choice) prior to reading this book, so while I was slightly prepared for the outcome, I figured I could find *something* to like.  Certainly Ms. Harris could be counted on to deliver a satisfying and exceptional conclusion for such an interesting heroine, right?<br /><br />Boy was I wrong.
