In my opinion, another major contribution to the film's reputation rests in an area often overlooked by the mainstream: its score, composed by Basil Poledouris, a longtime friend to Milius who went on to have a substantial but sadly-truncated career working with other directors like [[ASIN:B000VD5I94 Paul]] [[ASIN:B000UAFDP2 Verhoeven]] and [[ASIN:B001AII4SQ John McTiernan]] before succumbing to cancer in 2006.<br /><br />While Poledouris wrote other very fine works later in his career, including the epic mini-series Lonesome Dove, I believe that Conan the Barbarian is his magnum opus. Much like John Williams achieved with Star Wars, Poledouris structured the score in an operatic fashion, embedding the narrative within the music and maintaining a strength of composition so that much if not all of the music stands quite well on its own apart from the film. But while Williams filled his Star Wars scores full of Korngold-like flourishes that support the lighter atmosphere of those adventures, the more brutal and raw world of Conan required that Poledouris's music reflect this with a more focused and muscular sound.<br /><br />He delivered in spades. Unfortunately, the original recording has some caveats. While supervised and conducted by the composer, the film's budget made it necessary to record in Italy with the Orchestra & Chorus of Santa Cecilia, a smaller ensemble than Poledouris had originally envisioned to perform his music. The recording was not made in an ideal facility, quickly and on the cheap, and Poledouris had to modify the original orchestration to account for not only missing instruments but also performance issues. On the positive side, these realities lend the soundtrack recording a rawness that supports the filmic narrative; on the opposite, they don't necessarily make for an ideal listening experience.
