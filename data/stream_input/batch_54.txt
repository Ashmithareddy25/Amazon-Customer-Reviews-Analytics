It's almost appropriate that Joe Strummer is wearing an army helmet on the cover. If one puts either disc into their discman and then presses PLAY, they will hear that the band does indeed try to take everything on. They don't care if they're any good at that particular genre, they will play it with all the inimitable cynicism that makes them one of the best bands of the last 30 years.    That being said, since the Clash try to tackle everything all at once, not every song on this album works. But what does work works extremely well. For example, the band doesn't just write a disco song. They'll explore it from just about every conceivable angle, but not all at once. They'll try it one way, then try something else for a few songs, then come back to disco (but this time they might try combining it with something else just to see how it sounds). They do this with every genre they delve into. Ultimately, it's up to the listener to decide what's hot, and what's not.     Of the two discs, I would have to say Disc One is the most solid. I didn't like \\"The Magnificent Seven\\" the first time I heard it, but the second time, when I listened to the lyrics, I realized the Clash was maintaining their cynical sense of humor, spinning the working-man's tale over a garish, yet addictive disco-funk beat (\\"What do we have for entertainment? Cops kicking gypsies on the pavement.\\"). So in the end, \\"The Magnificent Seven\\" deserves to be called an essential Clash song. Sadly, I can't really say the same for \\"Hitsville UK,\\" which many other fans of the group consider to be a classic song. Maybe it's the fact that Mick Jones's girlfriend is singing backing vocals. I always felt that Jones never needed help singing, unless it came from Joe Strummer (the contrasts in their voices make for some amazing harmony). But anyway, I always skip this song. A few other songs of note on the first disc are:    -\\"Ivan Meets GI Joe.\\" I don't know why I like this song. I shouldn't like it as much as I do, but for some reason, it's one of my favorite songs on the album. I would say that again, the lyrics win me over. It's as if the band somehow takes the razzle-dazzle of a Roller Rink or Discotheque, and applies it to the threat of nuclear war between the superpowers of the time (\\"He tried his tricks, that Ruskie bear. United Nations said it's all fair\\").    -\\"Something About England.\\" I especially like the role-playing between Joe Strummer and Mick Jones on this one. I think the band, like a few other English musicians, saw the socio-economic troubles their country was entering at the start of the 1980s. Of course, what makes this song great is that Strummer's \\"old man\\" character is suggesting that such things are timeless.    -\\"Look Here.\\" This one is just catchy. It's like 1940s swing jazz with the unique spin of the Clash. For some reason, I keep thinking it could be a London Calling outtake.      -\\"Lightning Strikes (Not Once But Twice).\\" One of the many hybrid songs on the album. Paul Simonon's bass sounds distinctly funk, Topper Headon's drums sound distinctly hard rock, while Strummer growls with all the ferocity one might find in an early Clash song. This is perhaps one of the finest offerings on the album. Why it didn't make it onto The Essential Clash, I have no idea.     -\\"The Sound of Sinners.\\" The fact that the Clash are tackling gospel automatically makes this song ironic. And the icing on the cake is how blatant the lyrics are in their preaching of Old Testament wrath (\\"As the floods of God wash away Sin City, they say it was written in the page of the Lord. But I was looking for that great jazz note that destroyed the walls of Jericho\\"). Now imagine the preceding line being sung with Strummer's signature lispy growl.    Digressing for a moment, one of the most prolific sounds on here is reggae, which is experimented with in many forms. We have the wonderfully disjointed psychedelic bluegrass/reggae amalgam of \\"Junco Partner,\\" the hip-hop reggae of \\"One More Time\\" (which, coupled with the following track, \\"One More Dub,\\" just might be an early ancestor of modern hip-hop), and the blatant carnivale calypso of \\"Let's Go Crazy.\\" One gets a sense that even as far back as 1980, Joe Strummer discovered a sound that he seemed to enjoy, and would serve as a main influence when he started writing music again in 1999. Anyway, moving along to Disc Two:    This one starts out nice. The first five tracks are outstanding. We have the retro punk-rocker \\"Police On My Back,\\" followed by the soft, yet sharp country-rock of \\"Midnight Log.\\" Then things get a bit more experimental with \\"The Equaliser.\\" This was another song I didn't like when I first heard it, but by the third time, I realized that the group was blending a reggae bass-line with what ultimately sounds like disco played at a lower RPM. This only serves as a prelude to some of the more bizarre experimentation on the second half of the disc. \\"The Call Up\\" is a great R and B number with snatches of punk influence, but the real standout track of the second disc is \\"Washington Bullets.\\" The politics contained in the lyrics are a bit dated by the standards of today, but the simplicity of Strummer's rhyming, combined with the xylophone track being laid down by Headon, give the song the feel of a children's nursery rhyme (\\"Andin the Bay of Pigs in 1961, Havana fought a playboy in the Cuban sun. For Castro is a color is redder-than-red. Those Washington Bullets want Castro dead. Castro is a color that will earn you a spray of lead.\\") But once again, this just adds to the song's irony. Sadly, after track five, the second disc begins to fall apart. A few more highlights are \\"Charlie Don't Surf\\" (with its low-key Middle-Eastern influences), \\"Kingston Advice,\\" and \\"Version City.\\" But most of the rest of the disc consists of sampling earlier songs on the album, while adding layers of weird effects that ultimately don't serve any unique purpose. Or the band just does things that seem cheap and garish (such as having a child sing a cover of \\"Career Opportunities\\"). But as I said earlier, on an album of this scale, one can't expect everything to work.     So, in the end, and as the song says, \\"What have we got?\\" Well, according to a website I visited, the Clash were a band whose primary interest was sharing ideas with people. This album definitely contains an overwhelming amount of ideas, both lyrically and musically. I have yet to meet someone who likes every track, since there are thirty-six. But ultimately, if you find what works for you, then Sandinista! is a great album. It may not seem like it the first time you listen, but by the third time, hopefully you will realize that the Clash knew what they were doing, and after all is said and done, we are left with a work that might not be as legendary as London Calling, but still has a significant place in the Clash's catalog.
