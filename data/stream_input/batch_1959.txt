With all characters on a side so similar, it's easy to forget whether you're with Taim or Demandred, Elayne or Egwene.<br /><br />What Sanderson does do well here is meticulously describe the physical and channeling aspects of the Last Battle. He has a good head for detail and in all his works is good at making lists, drawing up rules of magic systems, etc. We are constantly kept apprised of who is dead and how they died, who is alive but in danger, where they all are, etc. That's harder than it sounds.<br /><br />And although Jordan warned us long ago that we wouldn't get answers to every niggling little detail, Sanderson's innate neatness compels him to be as helpful in providing answers as he can be. That some ofthe answers are now long past their expiration date, and they solve puzzles people stopped caring about in the 1990s, is not Sanderson's fault.<br /><br />This last, most cataclysmic volume seems a strange place to ramp up the slapstick, but Sanderson, who never quite got Mat right (though his Mat dialogue did improve greatly between [[ASIN:1841492329 The Gathering Storm]] and Towers of Midnight), apparently made the same decision regarding Mat's utility that Peter Jackson made about most of Tolkien's dwarves: that Mat is best used for undignified comic relief, by poking fun at his dress, speech, personal hygeine and intellect, and constantly pointing out English-language errors Mat makes when he writes. So naturally to emphasize this, Sanderson has Mat write a lot of letters, because it never gets old.<br /><br />Nor does the humorous incongruity of regal Elayne swearing like a particularly foul-mouthed stevedore in front of her easily shocked retainers ever pale, so Sanderson trots that same joke out before us repeatedly.<br /><br />I was somewhat puzzled by a trait I didn't notice Sanderson engage in in his first two WOT books (maybe he did and I just wasn't paying attention), and that is characters referring to everyone who is addressed as \\"Lord\\" or \\"Lady\\" (either by formal right to a title or just polite recognition of power) by such a title followed by their surname. Correct me if I'm wrong, and I know the manner of correct address varies by particular society and specific position, but typical under Jordan was for people to often say \\"Lord Dobriane,\\" \\"Lord Perrin,\\" or \\"Lady Faile;\\" not exclusively \\"Lord Aybara,\\" \\"Lord Mandragoran,\\" and the like. It's very strange once you notice it the first time, then you keep noticing it.<br /><br />A lot of battle scenes, not as much suspense / revelation as in some of the earlier books. Under Sanderson, some characters, not just Androl and Pevara, assumed new importance, while others such as Min and Nynaeve saw their roles greatly shrink. And where Jordan saw the characters as still important to each other even when they weren't specifically fixed on an important task, the example of the way Thom was abruptly thrust to the back burner once his rescue of Moiarine was done exemplifies Sanderson's too-utilitarian view of characters.<br /><br />Given the nature of the Bond, it's understandable but still a little too convenient that everyone significant who dies seems to die in pairs: Egwene and Gawyn, Siuan and Gareth, Bashere and Deira (not Bonded). Galad and Lan don't die because Berleian and Nynaeve don't. I'm not sorry I invested in either work, and the journey along each was a decent reward despite the meager payoff at their ends. But for both, you had to be there at the time to appreciate the phenomena. Most of the fun about WOT was theorizing endlessly as the series progressed in real time. I couldn't in good conscience recommend WOT to anyone from this moment forward, any more than I endorse Lost anymore, because their days are already done, even WOT's.<br /><br />It's like The Simpsons' episode where Homer discovers Lost in 2012 and wants to discuss theories - but only through the middle of season four. Unless a new WOT reader finds many new WOT readers with whom to experience the series for the first time at the same pace, and they try to recreate The Wheel theory universe as was done in the 1990s and 2000s, plowing through 14 books a good half of which are unproductive, they won't get out of it what those of us who read Book 13 when it first came out, or Book 11, or the excellent [[ASIN:187937174X Book 6]], etc. did.<br /><br />The long-awaited, underwhelming revelation about Demandred's whereabouts is also straight out of the Lost playbook - they should have told us five books ago if they wanted people to really care. And like the \\"Who Killed Asmodean?\\" riddle, the Where's Berid Bel? puzzle too much depended on a weak process of elimination - since it wasn't anyone else in the whole opus, it must have been Graendal. Since Demandred isn't with anyone who's not the Sharans, he must be in Shara. Faulty reasoning, and imagine a prosecutor trying to get a convicition with reasoning like that. But the two authors seem to find laborious revelation by negation to be powerful proof.<br /><br />The sudden emergence out of nowhere of the Sharans as the main earthly foe is straight out of E.E. \\"Doc\\" Smith's space opera - have your heroes fight the villains for decades, then at the end suddenly reveal that new supersecret supervillains behind the supposed villains are the real threat. To be fair, Jordan did long ago have Sammael and Graendal briefly explain to us how channeling worked in Sharan society. Still, for the typical reader to intuit then that nine books later that minor exchange would be the key to the series was a bit much to expect.<br /><br />Remember that puzzled feeling some of us has when first finishing up [[ASIN:B002VBV1HC The Eye of the World]]? \\"Wait, what just happened? Who did he kill, that Dark One? Is it over?\\" And the same authorially deliberate uncertainty came at the end of [[ASIN:B0073WSQHM The Dragon Reborn]] - \\"So this Ba'alzamon fellow was the Dark One, and now he's dead, and The Wheel of Time trilogy is over, right?\\"<br /><br />But in those cases the uncertainty was because we were following along with Rand, and Rand wasn't yet fully up to speed - in part because of natural ignorance, and in part because Moiraine was still sheltering him from the full truth. Because now Rand does seem to get what occurred. His thoughts are just less well-explained.<br /><br />I see where the Eddings comparison in one review is coming from - Sanderson did write the series much more Twilighty and tween than Jordan did. He doesn't come across as a dumb or immature guy, but his fiction definitely seems to aim at a young adult level. I enjoy the [[ASIN:076536543X Mistborn]] books for what they are (and again, his logical magical systems rather than characterization are his strength), but they're no Jordan WOT. Most Jordan women always had a bit of Polgara in them anyway, and in Sanderson hands they become even more judgmental and humorless than they were under Jordan.<br /><br />As for the title of my piece, it is not 100% a joke or throwaway cynicism to wonder if there was a deliberate setting up of an eventual successor series here. There is enough left unresolved and unexplained that I would be unshocked if in 2016, whether it is planned already or not, TOR Books, the estate of Robert Jordan and Brandon Sanderson announced the forthcoming publication of \\"A New Age Brings New Winds: The First Volume of The Children of the Dragon.\\"<br /><br />In particular the whole future of the Aiel / remnant of a remnant / prey of the Seanchan subplot was left unresolved. Yet in this climactic volume Sanderson was still spending a decent amount of time on Aviendha and the other Foolish Ones discussing the importance of her descendant vision, Amys's confirmation of same, theneed for second and third, (and why not fourth and fifth?) trips to Rhuidean, and in general how the future of a post-Battle Seanchan tyranny can be averted.<br /><br />Because all signs are that it WON'T be averted unless someone actively averts it. Rand did not bring the Seanchan to heel. He did kneel before the Crystal Throne, validating in their minds their right to subjugate all other peoples, enslave channelers, and in general be beastly to everyone. The Seanchan version of who deserves to rule where and how will prevail. And with Moghedien subsumed into Seanchan, their slavery-based society isn't going to get spontaneously better.<br /><br />Mat suggested that Hawkwing have a talk with Tuon, but did it happen? How did it go? Did Tuon or her people realize Rand was right that the whole world doesn't revolve around them?<br /><br />Rand made Tuon sign a piece of paper, but Rand is \\"dead,\\" and without the Dragon's power behind them, the remnant of a remnant Aiel probably won't be able to stave off the empire.<br /><br />Far from his freeing the slaves the Seanchan did take or keeping them from taking more slaves, Rand actually left Min in their violent hands; raised to honor, but not free to leave.<br /><br />And what about Aviendha's possible children, and the Dragon's other children? There's a lot of scope there for a follow-up if Harriet and/or Tom Doherty start missing their cash cow series, even if Sanderson were to prefer to continue to develop his own worlds.<br /><br />The foregoing is all part of why I suspect the series may have deliberately been written less conclusively than it could have been.<br /><br />All in all, I'm glad it's over for now. I'm sure each of us had our own versions of how we'd like the WOT to end (I know that a lot of people insisted Rand had to be dead and stay dead for the series to be fulfilling, for instance), and I think very few of us had as our dream ending Rand winning by vague unspecified means, then cheerfully going offon wanderjahr alone. But presumably this is the ending the series was always going to have, such as it is.
