0765348780|4.0|Summarizing a novel like Gardens of the Moon is a daunting task (or as Tool would say, \\"futile\\"). Perhaps just as daunting is actually reading it, which is at times obtuse to the point that less patient readers are sure to give up before the first hundred pages have been 'surmounted'.<br /><br />Do not be fooled by the book's length, either, which at first appears standard for epic fantasy. The novel's density is unparalleled in the genre, with a confounding mixture of detailed, but at times obscure prose. In fact, Erikson's writing often eschews exposition in favor of concise descriptions of character and action, with a tendency to ponder the philosophical ramifications of the events and psychologies of his many characters. If you're looking for casual, light reading, look elsewhere.<br /><br />Unlike most works in the epic fantasy canon, Gardens of the Moon begins in the midst of a great conflict between opposing forces, with the Malazan Empire, under the rule of the Empress Laseen, threatening to conquer the remaining cities of the continent of Genabackis. Much of the novel's first half revolves around the siege of the city of Pale and its aftermath, with events in Darujhistan, the last free city, covering the second half. Throughout, readers should expect to have characters, place names, and bits of lore thrown at them at breakneck speed, especially in the first half. Observant readers will find subtle hints as to what's going on, but it's almost a guarantee that nearly every first-time reader will be lost in the layered, complex storytelling at various points. Perseverance, and perhaps even some note-taking is key to enjoying this work to its fullest, as there is little hand-holding to be found here.<br /><br />To describe the storyline as 'complex' would be a great understatement. The plethora of characters, events, histories, perspectives, and lore are such that one could probably choose another fantasy series in its entirety, and find that Gardens of the Moon alone manages to exceed it in terms of detail. Additionally, Erikson takes many of the conventions and tropes associated with the genre, and undermines them in a way that is at times sly, and other times surreal. Even the dreaded deus ex machina device is used to distort our understanding of the world, working simultaneously as a sort of parody. There are also numerous references to other works of literature, ranging from Greek mythology to Vonnegut. Nevertheless, it's easy to see how many could find the novel incomprehensible and pretentious, rather than immersive and 'fun'.<br /><br />You might wonder; 'is it worth the trouble?' Yes, but as with most works in the genre, there are some caveats that need to be observed before taking the plunge, for the waters here are deep. First and foremost is Erikson's prose, which has a flavor that is more 'literary' and less transparent than that of most fantasy authors. There is a grittiness and moral ambiguity here that grounds the characters and action in a way that feels authentic, and is similar to Martin's Ice and Fire novels. Unlike that series' low-magic world, Erikson's is rife with magic, fantastical creatures (many of which are intelligent), gods, demi-gods, and powerful artifacts. Additionally, displays of god-like powers are common, with epic battles being waged by high mages, demons, dragons, and so on. The descriptions of these battles and their scope is impressive to say the least, though I couldn't help but feel at certain points that the novel's sheer ambition seemed to buckle somewhat under its own (incredible) weight. The reasoning for this is elusive, but there were times when the tone of the novel felt inconsistent, as if Erikson's many influences didn't always converge in an agreeable manner. At one moment, for example, we may be experiencing a small, intimate moment with a character as they muse on one of the novels many themes, with a decidedly reserved and mature atmosphere contained therein. Suddenly, within the same chapter, we switch to another character's POV, in which an action sequence or bit of hammy dialogue might undermine said atmosphere, sometimes coming across as a page out of a pulp fantasy novel. The effect is jarring, since there are moments of subtle brilliance sprinkled throughout the text (sections that language geeks will want to mull over in contemplation), followed by the aforementioned 'over-the-top' action scenes. While this phenomenon is not indicative of the novel's entirety, it occurred enough to make the book's many threads at times feel more frayed than refined.<br /><br />Detractors also like to point to the novel's supposed propensity for a large cast of 'shallow' characters, written with minimal characterization. While Erikson doesn't explore his character's psychology to the extent that Martin does (few authors do, in fact), to call them generic would simply be false. Far from being shallow, characters like Paran, Whiskeyjack, Tattersail, Lorn, Crokus, and Kruppe are multifaceted and compelling, with complex motivations that go beyond simplistic good and evil posturing. Again, Erikson relies more on dialogue and action than exposition, leaving readers to interpret the characters and events at their leisure.<br /><br />Gardens of the Moon is surely an acquired taste, whose outright inaccessibility will prove too much of a roadblock for some, but ultimately its greatness lies in its willingness to go beyond the confines of 'ordinary' fantasy. Readers who pay attention to detail and take their time doing so will be rewarded in the end, since despite being the first entry in a ten-book series, it stands admirably on its own. Many of the plot threads and character arcs are wrapped up by the end, with strands leftover for future installments.<br /><br />At times brilliant, frustrating, surreal and bizarre, the novel is, if nothing else, ambitious in scope, style, and theme. It's not without its shortcomings, but thankfully, most of them are simply overwhelmed by its many strengths. It is often compared to other works of 'mature' fantasy, but even having only read the first entry thus far, I am convinced that Erikson's voice is unique, if peculiar. Recommended for patient, detail-oriented readers.
