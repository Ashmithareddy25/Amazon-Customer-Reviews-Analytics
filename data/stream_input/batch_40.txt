I'm still trying to figure out which application techniques worked best, and so far it was my hood, where I laid it on thick, smoothed it out quickly, smoothed out the excess again few hours later (wasn't quite &#34;wiping it off&#34;--just the lightest, straight strokes with a microfiber), then washed very gently the next day (I think it could be sooner with this technique. After initial thick application, the excess (or solvent?) actually beads up a bit on itself after awhile (looked a little like dew on the car, only there was no dew), which is what I'm talking about smoothing out/wiping down a 2nd time. When I say 'wipe', I mean almost no pressure. I then washed VERY GENTLY the next day, but feel it could possibly be straightaway, and that was it. I used that well-reviewed turtle ice wash-n-wax, but doubt the 'wax' part did much (it sure didn't do much on my bare paint section). Car car videos say to not use pressure washers or dish soap, as they remove wax, and I want this stuff to stay on as long as possible. Anyway, after washing, the results were better than I expected for the effort, but then had those streaks in some sections a day or two later after it was fully dried out. I will now use a &#34;wax it dry&#34; products on top instead, to complete a super-easy routine I think I'll actually do.  So what do I know. I'm not really into this as a hobby, but if you're reading this far, you probably aren't either. Please realize I'm experimenting to find something of maximal ease for people who don't want to do this. Although I'm thinking now that a simple &#34;wax-as-you-dry&#34; product may be easier (it's basically 1 step, this is not), but those probably don't last as long. Now that I have this, it remains to be seen if it's synergistic with other products like wax-dry's; I assume it will be.  The reason I wanted to try putting Paint Protect on thick, was because it seemed to me that people who had bad experiences with this, seemed to be using it thinner. Plus, who doesn't want more protection, right? The fact mine took more than a day to dry out shows me I either used more than the directions specify, or didn't take enough off. Again, that was basically of on purpose, and so far the thickest part (my hood) actually looks the best. YMMV.  As applicators, I tried using a virgin foam bonnet, a virgin microfiber applicator (the puffy kind with foam inside, both from wally world), and even tried a clean plastic bag over my hand. I think anything works, you just have to smoosh it around and get it fairly even, but the bag didn't stay on my hand well enough. I write this to point out you don't have to have skill or special equipment to use it, though I do think microfiber towels are huge advancements for car care. This product is easy enough that women, children, and even elderly I think could use it, with some instruction (included instructions are inadequate IMO). I would say washing a car (properly, and by hand) is actually significantly HARDER, and more time-consuming, than using this product. And I think that is the purpose of this product. So if you can wash a car, you can use this. There are &#34;better&#34; products--but perhaps not ones easier to use which are more forgiving. (When I say &#34;forgiving&#34;, I mean not ending up with an irreversible disaster like my hardened wax episodes, I do not mean consistency of result.)  I agree with reviewers that the product is a dust magnet, though I haven't used products which &#34;repel&#34; dust either.  WHAT I'VE FOUND When the results are easy and gratifying, you actually want to do it more. I never thought I'd say this, but I now see how some guys can get so into it that they look for excuses to wax their car/try a different product. Also found that bare paint is harder to wash and keep clean. If washing my car needs less effort, I'll do it more often. I also suspect now that what paint care products mean by &#34;UV protection&#34; may actually be that having a coating on the paint is protecting it from oxidation, which may be catylized by UV rays. I saw a video of a lab worker with a UV meter trying several popular products, and with his technique anyway, none actually blocked significant if any UV. But I don't think that matters. It's keeping the oxygen off, especially if exposed to sunlight.  I was raised to believe Wash-n-wax and wax-it-dry products were the devil, but after reading a review of a retired 30-year detailer, who only uses such products on his own car due to physical limitations, it opened my mind. Maybe &#34;Easy that you'll do&#34;, is better than &#34;Best that you won't&#34;.  I'm not looking for the best cosmetic look. There are plenty of flaws in my paint, and they're still visible up-close. But from a distance, it actually looks great, and we'll see how easily I can maintain it, or how long it'll last if I don't. Most importantly, I need any solution to be easy on my hands & wrists.  I left a small portion of paint uncovered, and this was a very good reality check as to what the product's doing, and to monitor if it's really there.  TRIM & RUBBER This product is awesome on non-glass surfaces like faded plastic trim. Some people use things like peanut oil to darken oxidized trim, which is stupid, because oil degrades plastic. This stuff acts similar to what I imagine peanut oil does, but I hope will not be harmful (there is petroleum in it, I just hope it all evaporates). People have reported good results on rubber. I plan to try some 303 protectant, followed by this stuff, since 303 is water-soluble, and Paint Protect really isn't.  I gave this product 3 reviews over 4 because of how different the results can look, depending on how it's applied. When it works, it's great. When it doesn't work, it looks pretty 'fail' up-close in bright sunlight, although the protection is definitely still there. I'll continue to experiment and update here if I learn more. Attractive or not, application is EASY, the tough part is just knowing which application method will look and protect the best.
