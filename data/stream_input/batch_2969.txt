Dan Torrance drifts around the eastern United States, keeping as far away from the Rockies as he can while drowning both the traumatic events of his childhood and his unique telepathic gift in a flood of cheap whiskey and barroom brawls.  After hitting rock-bottom in a Wilmington, North Carolina apartment, he winds up in Frazier, New Hampshire, a tourist-trap town in the White Mountains where he befriends a few locals and gets himself into AA.  He also lands a job with a local hospice where he, along with the help of a true story-inspired prescient cat, Azreel, aids the more seriously ill patients during the final countdown.  Dubbed \\"Dr. Sleep,\\" he has just about turned his life around totally when he befriends a young girl named Abra Stone.<br /><br />At only thirteen, Abra (yes, as in \\"cadabra\\") has the shining too, but is already stronger than Dan ever was.  Cause for celebration, to be sure, except Abra's gift has attracted the attention of some very scary non-people.  The True Knot is a Manson Family-esque gang of murderous supernatural gypsies who travel the country in a variety of RVs and motorhomes, feeding off the essence of children who have the shining.  Led by the beautiful, psychotic Rose the Hat and her Shakespeare-quoting lover Crow Daddy, the True Knot are essentially psychic vampires who were once all-powerful, but their numbers are dwindling.  Believing that feeding on Abra will restore them to their once near-invincible strength, the True Knot targets our young heroine and it is up to Dan to help her in the fight against them.<br /><br />First of all, let's talk about what worked.  Short answer: a lot.  I loved meeting back up with Dan and finding out that he still hasn't really escaped the Overlook.  Some might find this depressing, I found it realistic in regards to anyone who has been through such a traumatic experience, especially at such a young age. Seeing Dan hit rock-bottom was truly heartbreaking, and the scene illustrating this end to the downward spiral is possibly among the best King has ever written. I also loved the sprawling story it told; whereas \\"The Shining\\" is an exercise in claustrophobic horror, \\"Dr. Sleep\\" ping-pongs across the country and actually becomes somewhat of a road-trip story for part of the second act and nearly all of the third.  Abra Stone is probably the most realistic child character King has written since first creating Danny; while some of his more recent kid characters--such as Joe McClatchey and Norry Calvert in \\"Under the Dome\\"--seem one-dimensional and overly-precocious, Abra is a tough, crafty little chick who does not annoy the reader or impede the narrative whatsoever.  In fact, under her girl-next-door charm beats the heart of a truly intimidating adversary for our villains, Rose and her tribe of immortals.<br /><br />Speaking of the TrueKnot, I honestly loved these guys.  While their actions are truly despicable, King does a great job of developing their lifestyle and dynamic, managing to make them even somewhat likable.  We don't sympathize with them really, but we at least understand why they do what they do.  They are also, for better or worse, a family, and care about each other as much as the novel's more morally straight characters.  Rose the Hat is deliciously evil without being over-the-top, and Crow Daddy is one smooth-talkin' no-nonsense devil.  Fans of Rob Zombie's movie \\"The Devil's Rejects\\" will get a kick out this bunch of beer-gutted and polyestered nomadic serial killers.<br /><br />As for what didn't work: a few things.  One of the bad things about releasing a sequel almost forty years after its predecessor is the writer's fear that the reader will not remember what happened in the first book.  Considering \\"The Shining\\"'s popularity, I'm honestly a little surprised King was afraid of this, but this authorial anxiety is definitely evident in \\"Dr. Sleep,\\" given the amount of times King references the Overlook Hotel, certain scenes from the first novel, etc.  They grow somewhat tiresome, but luckily there's enough new material to soften the blow of these \\"hey, remember whens?\\"  While the book kicks off to an exciting start and has a truly satisfying conclusion, it does bog down a bit in the middle as Dan works in the hospice while Abra figures out how to use her powers.  A few more nit-picky things that are by no means foreign to a King book: at times clunky dialogue, schmaltzy character interactions, occasionally repetitive plot points, etc.  Honestly though, this book's biggest flaw has nothing to do with quality, the story, or the characters: it's just not \\"The Shining.\\"<br /><br />Is this a new classic? No.  Is it as good as \\"The Shining\\"? No.  Is it the best thing King has ever written? Definitely not.  But, if you can get past your own nostalgia, you'll realize that there is still a great story being told here, the work of a master who, despite all the naysayers and cynics, is still firing on all cylinders.  Give this book a buy, and go on yet another adventure with Dan Torrance, the kid--now man--who shines on.
