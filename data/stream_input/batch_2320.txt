Owen first travels to Everville in an astral like manner, thinking at first this is all a dream. He is eventually told by The Keeper who brought him to Everville that he has been chosen to help save Everville from the mysterious \\"Them\\" and his own world by extension, and begins to train in both our world and in Everville for his task.<br /><br />The novel began well enough, but about chapter three I began to get an uneasy feeling about its plot development, character development, and intended direction.<br /><br />I especially found the first half of the novel hard to get through. I had to stop several times to vent frustration I felt as a reader about several moments of unrealized potential and a few anti-climactic scene cuts where a scene would end just before something interesting was about to happen, or it would simply summarize or skip over a part of the scene that I had been led to believe was the direction of the scene due to its build, momentum, and structure.<br /><br />Frustration also came early on in the form of the characters. Characters in general were hard to imagine as Mr. Huff attaches one or two adjectives to the characters and then summarily dismisses any further description. It's often been a critique of romanticism and literature influence by romanticism (like epic fantasy) that they exist in a world where life exists mostly from the neck up with little concern for the neck down. For most of the novel we're trapped inside the head of Owen, how much more \\"neck up\\" can you get besides that? The statement about being concerned mostly with life from the neck up is quite true of Everville: The First Pillar as there is little to no concern for the bodies and physical actions of our characters outside of the three chapters concerning the roundabout and training for it. I still have no clue what Anika or Dante look like (a tremendous problem as both are rather important characters), nor what a marmamook looks like beyond that it's furry, has hands, and eyes (another tremendous problem as this creature is a pivotal character for nearly half the novel). Mr. Huff when in Everville describes the landscapes quite well, but I would ask of him to please devote some time to giving his important characters descriptions equaling his landscape descriptions at the very least.<br /><br />Besides physical description I could have used some further character development, most especially concerning the characters of Anika and Dante whose biggest distinguishing factors as characters are that one is a male friend of Owen's and the other is a female friend of Owen's. There is hardly any development of them as characters beyond that, and their reactions are usually the same and often conveyed in near unison. I never got the sense that either were truly individual characters in their own right, which is a shame considering that they're Owen's best friends since childhood.<br /><br />The setting of being at college becomes less important and less realistic over the course of the first half of the novel. Owen never suffers any consequences in terms of his college classes due to his trips to Everville. In fact he doesn't have a roommate (highly unbelievable that a college freshman in an old New England university wouldn't have a roommate). He also never attends any college classes outside of his one elective of Art History that we see, they are mentioned but they hold no weight or influence on the plot of the novel. I don't expect to see a complete representation of all his college classes, but some acknowledgment that they exist outside of the first quarter of the novel would be nice. This also makes me question why have this novel take place at college if the atmosphere of a college isn't utilized to its fullest. Perhaps his trips to Everville causes him to miss out on writing a college paper or something else, or he takes an astral trip to Everville during the middle of class causing him to miss information that causes his grades to suffer or something like that. Most of the novel is devoted to Owen's participation in college extracurricular activities which are really training for his time to come in Everville disguised as campus clubs. And that's nice, but why does this have to take place at a college then? Couldn't the clubs just as easily be exclusive private clubs and associations of our actual society or of Owen's job, and Owen's a college graduate looking to make his way in the real world? Why have the story take place at a college if its atmosphere isn't going to be important beyond the first quarter of the novel?<br /><br />In terms of anti-climactic moments, besides the sudden scene changes that cut-off promising scenes before they start or skip over them to a later time, I found myself most unhappy with how The Pillar was handled itself. I can see what Mr. Huff is trying to achieve, that it's a great neutral force of nature in the world, which is an element typical of most romances (like The Green Knight of Arthurian romance who is neither good nor evil). However the sense I got from it was that it could solve all the problems of the novel if it so inclined to do so. I would have appreciated a character venting this frustration just once before finally accepting the proclamation of the pillar. In addition, I found Owen's sacrifice and then the reversal of that sacrifice by the pillar to be very anti-climactic and only add to this idea that the pillar could, if it wanted defeat \\"Them\\" with a wave of its hand. Similarly Carwyn's encounter with \\"Them\\"'s army sends your heartbeat racing with anticipation only to leave you with a let down of a disengaging of potential conflict. The army simply letting Carwyn go after failing to convince him to join \\"Them\\" seemed unrealistic and very much was a let down in terms of a building climax, and made me think less of \\"Them\\" as the \\"ultimate evil\\". Also there is a lot of build up to taking a trip to the Hamptons, with a whole subplot devoted to Owen winning a competition that allows them to go. The trip turns out to be very unimportant in terms of the plot and much of a let down comes in the form that the trip only lasts one single chapter, when I was half expecting the last quarter of the novel to take place at the Hamptons mansion we'd fought to get to.<br /><br />Another large problem is that the story spends most of its time in narrating and commenting on the actions of the characters and plot from a distance instead of actively showing these things. It would be much more powerful if we saw how the cave dwellers were negative and pessimistic--a caustic few lines would do, if we were allowed to see for ourselves Anika's dislike of Drusilla in her relationship and interactions with her character, etc. These are just two examples of many more that I could list, but showing these things instead of telling us about them would greatly improve their value to the novel and enhance it overall.<br /><br />Misspellings, awkward transitions (both narratively and grammatically), a tendency for anti-climactic solutions to conflict, and heterographs (like confusing hoard for horde) are Mr. Huff's main weaknesses. I'd advise him in the future to engage in a more thorough and extensive proofreading before releasing his work to the public. Perhaps going so far as to join a group of writers and seek support from fellow peers in the field.<br /><br />What was so frustrating for me though was I could see so much potential for this story that lay undeveloped. And the existence of that undeveloped potential frustrated me to no end with this story time and again and yet inspired me to hope that maybe it might come through in the end. I give the two stars I have for the undeveloped potential that lays there in the novel, but has yet to come shining forth. I hope Mr. Huff in the future considers revising this novel, because I think it could only benefit from such an examination.
