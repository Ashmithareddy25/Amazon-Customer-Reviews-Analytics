1400049628|1.0|It's really difficult to know where to begin when critiquing a work of fiction so commonly lauded as Max Brooks' Zombie Survival Guide. I first read the book a few years ago, but recently I got a copy (probably the same one) from the library, and since I already knew the portions I liked (and the far more numerous ones I didn't), I was able to do a far more concentrated analysis, rather than a more thorough slog. As such, this review isn't going to be as thorough as some others available, but it will still hit what I think are the highpoints, as well as the more major low ones.<br />First off, there's Brooks' assertion that bladed weapons are better suited to fighting the undead, because blades don't need to be reloaded. Ignoring the fact that machetes and other sharp melee weapons (especially Brooks' favored katana) dull and break, there are other concerns as well. First off, you're closing with an enemy that can only hurt you from about two feet away, whereas a gun lets you stay at a safe distance. Then there's the fact that it takes a lot of strength to sever a human neck (to quote Frightnight, \\"Bone is a mother\\" lover). We can also bypass the obvious psychological difficulties associated with decapitation, and go right for the meat of the matter: this method is irresponsible. Which, after all, is easier to keep track of? A full body zombie, or a single, reanimated but disembodied head? You aren't actually stopping the infected, you're just leaving it behind (something like a landmine) for the next poor fool to stumble across. And unlike you, he won't know it's there.<br />Brooks is a major proponent of melee weapons, although he does allow for the use of guns. Since firearms aren't readily available, I'll grant him this; I'd advocate carrying both two firearms and a fire ax, pick, or hammer. However, the chapter on guns is so poorly researched as to boggle my mind. Breaking weapons down into categories, Brooks starts off with heavy machine guns, which he rejectsout of hand for the logical reason that headshots are difficult to get on full-auto. Unfortunately, the weapon Brooks uses as an example is the M249 Squad Automatic Weapon, a 5.56mm (.223 caliber) Light Machine Gun, and nowhere near as powerful as Brooks makes it out to be. Equally baffling is his summary rejection of assault rifles, due to the temptation of going full auto (apparently Brooks leaves in a world where automatic weapons are easy to come by in America). Brooks cites the M16A1 as the worst assault rifle in human history (further stating the M16A2 isn't much better), while the AK-47 is the best weapon ever made. This is hilarious, since, while the AK is surely a rugged weapon, it's also a supremely poorly designed weapon when it comes to accuracy (the Israeli Galil, which Brooks cites as a perfect copy, has its iron sights situated better for aimed shooting, rather than massed, automatic volley fire like the Soviets originally went for). Semi-auto rifles and bolt actions are given the logical thumbs up (although oddly enough, Brooks rates the M1 Garand the best of this class, rather than the newer and more common M14 and AR-15 series rifles). Shotguns are just about as useless as assault rifles (unless you use the strategy Brooks ripped right out of Resident Evil and mass multiple zombies together, then decapitate them all in one shot, something which is impossible in real life). SMGs are also dismissed, despite both the fact that they fire, by definition, pistol-caliber ammo (and are therefore fairly easy to keep loaded), can mount lots of optics, tend to have higher capacities than rifles, and are also illegal to own in automatic (at which point they become a semi-auto handgun with a stock). Handguns are relegated to being last-ditch weapons (something I would switch with the machete). Even more confusingly, .22 Rimfire weapons are treated as not only a separate class, but are in fact preferred, due to the relatively small size of their projectiles making for more carrying space. This ignores the fact that .22s are horribly inaccurate, extremely prone to misfires and duds, and on top of that are extremely loud (something not so great for evading the undead). Given that Brooks dismissed the 5.56mm M16 (because ammo for the American assault rifles so prevalent in America is apparently sold only to the military in Brooks' world), even though the 5.56 cartridge is .001 inches bigger in diameter, extremely reliable, and accurate to over a mile. It isn't especially necessary to come up with an entirely new virus, when he could've taken the Romero approach and simply accept the audience has suspended its disbelief, or better yet, come up with several different approaches to dealing with different zombies (from Romero to voodoo to genetically engineered infected). Instead, we get an extremely boring chapter that has no bearing on anything, on top of which introduces a zombie virus with a completely asinine name (Solanum? Seriously?). Dull. Simply put, it isn't funny(or at least not intentionally), and that ought to be the final nail in the coffin (or bullet to the head) of this truly worthless book.
