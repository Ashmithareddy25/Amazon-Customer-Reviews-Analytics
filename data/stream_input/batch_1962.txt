1400067936|4.0|Having reviewed Taleb's new book Antifragile (Taleb, 2012), I was encouraged to go back and also review his earlier book Fooled by Randomness (Taleb, 2004). As with my previous review, I ask three questions: `what is the book about, what can we learn from it and what do I think of it'? Why these three questions? They help me think in terms of the objective (what is `it'), the inter-subjective (what can `we' learn from it) and the subjective (what do `I' think about it), i.e., the three domains of knowledge that philosophers have long thought to be present in each and every phenomenon (Kant, Plato, Torbert, Wilber).<br /><br /> What is it? The central theme of the book is that we are Fooled by Randomness. We are not wired, says Taleb, to understand probabilities and thus we underestimate the share of randomness in almost everything. This is primarily due to a lack of appreciation of how our emotions rather than our thinking influence our actions. The main point is that estimating probabilities in `symmetric' environments (such as in a coin toss) is completely different to estimating probabilities in an `asymmetric' environment were the odds payoffs are different (as in the stock market). Taleb, by his own admission, comes from the `tragic view of mankind' side of the house as opposed to the utopian view of mankind as proposed by Rousseau, Thomas Paine and others. The tragic view of mankind believes there are inherent limitations and flaws in the way we think and act. His major influencers include: Karl Hopper [scientific falsification and distrust of intellectual answers, actually of anyone who is confident that he knows anything with certainty]. Frederick Hayek and Milton Friedman [suspicious of governments], Adam Smith [intentions of man], Herbert Simon [bounded rationality], Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman [heuristics and biases], speculator George Soros, philosopher Charles Sanders Pierce and perhaps most important John Maynard Keynes [Treatise on Probability].<br /><br /> What can we learn from it? A few things stand out; a) that random events happen more often than we think, b) that is doesn't matter how often you win if a large loss can wipe you out, i.e., a black swan event, c) that we don't `intuitively' manage probabilistic thinking very well - it requires discipline, d) that we are wired to react emotionally to uncertainty and thus need cognitive tricks to help us avoid it, e) that our cognitive apparatus generally plays an inferior role to our emotional machinery in directing our actions, f) that we cannot judge a performance in a given field from the result - we need to know how the game was played. Why?  Because certainty is something that is likely to take place across the highest number of different alternative histories, uncertainty concerns events that should take place in the lowest number of them. Improving our probabililty thinking is about identifying the `uncertain' events, the invisible risks that might harm us.  Taleb uses a monte carlo simulator to help him generate these alternative paths, g) that the impact of holding debt is asymmetric; a small drop in the market is rapidly multiplied by leverage - how well has this lesson been learned in recent years, both when markets went up and down?<br />Early in the book he provides a list of `tensions' which he calls his `table of confusion' and says that, often people think they are talking about the former when in fact they are referring to the later, i.e., `skills and luck, `historical determinism and randomness', `certainty and probability', knowledge and belief', `reality and theory', `casuality and anecdote', `prophecy and forecasts'. He also highlights `a lack of understanding of standard error and critical thinking' and how `probability is a qualitative subject' by which he means that what matters most in probability is not the quantitative statistical method used but the qualitative `assumptions' underpinning ones approach to it.<br /><br /> What doI think of it? I am pleased to be reminded of the core concepts of the book. When I first read Fooled by Randomness in 2006, I didn't fully appreciate the difference between the qualitative and quantitative aspects of probability; I was assuming a quantitative only approach. I am also not sure I really understood the `inductive problem', i.e., going from the particular to the general. This becomes much clearer when put in the context of behavioural science and how heuristics and biases influence decisions under uncertainty. In this context it is useful to read Kahneman's (2012) latest book Thinking Fast Thinking Slow. I am also pleased to be re-introduced to the scepticism of David Hume and to Karl Popper who both addressed the issues of inference and the induction problem. For Popper there are two types of theories; those that have been proven wrong (falsified) and those that have yet to be proven wrong (falsifiable). How much closer can we get to the truth if we concentrate on `falsification' as opposed to `verification' and how could we improve ourselves and our research by offering up conditions in which our theories and results could be rejected? Charlie Munger (Warren Buffett's business partner of 40 years), who also comes from the doom and gloom side of the house, says that until you are able to argue the case against yourself you are not ready to go forward. In my own experience I have found this kind of `falsification' very difficult. As I observe myself, I am draw to `confirming' as opposed to `disconfirming' evidence of my underlying view, whatever it is.<br /><br /> The standout learning for me however is Taleb's highlighting of the confusion between `probability and expectation' in Chapter 6, `Skewness and Asymmetry'. That is the first thing. The second thing is what has it caused me to do? Perhaps the easiest way of explaining this is to assume you are making a bet where the probability of winning or losing and the gain from winning or loosing are notthe same. In this case the environment is `asymmetric'. This is by way of contrasting a `symmetric' environment, such as a coin-toss, where the odds are even and the winner takes all. Warren Buffett describes this issue as follows: Buffett (1989)  says, that in estimating the probability of an event (could be a bet at the races) you should, \\"Take the probability of gain times the amount of the possible gain from the probability of loss times the amount of the loss\\". This is another way of saying, `take the weighted outcome of the probable and improbable outcomes'. For instance, in a bet where you have the same chance to win $200 as loose $100, the `weighted outcome' is $50 which is in your favour, whereas the other way around the weighted outcome would be -$50 which is not in your favour in which case it would not make sense to take the bet. This may seem obvious, but it is nonetheless the kind of simple mistake of reason that can lead to a sub-optimal probability decisions. The other key point is to `do your own estimates', i.e., don't take the odds offered by the track or the prices quoted on the exchange - do your own estimates of the probable and improbable outcomes and then look at what prices are on offer. If the weighted outcome is your favour then you can consider proceeding, if not' then don't. Of course you can't do this unless you know something about the environment you are in, hence Buffett's definition of risk being \\"not knowing what you are doing\\".<br /><br /> This has caused me to reflect on whether this simple approach to estimating the `weighted outcome' can get over the inductive problem and the extent to which `heuristics and biases' occupy the path. I was thinking this morning for instance on a bet on a forthcoming game of Rugby (partly as a way to improve my probabilistic thinking) and I wondered where the information to make the decision was coming from? I noticed a few things; firstly I wasn't taking a very disciplined approach to working out the probabilities. Secondly, I hadn't considered that I was operating in an `asymmetric' environment and what that meant, i.e., that I needed to do more work on the `alternative histories' in assessing the risk. Thirdly, I don't think I have sufficiently acknowledged that in `asymmetric environments' that are certain things that are `unknowable' and so even after working out the probabilities you are still taking a `punt' so to speak. (Buffett would cover this aspect of risk taking by having a sufficiently large `Margin of Safety'). Fourthly, this approach also has some consequences, i.e., that I am likely to turn down many more bets than make them. I was presupposing I was `taking the bet' whatever the odds - this more disciplined approach is likely to stop you making those kind of `entertaining' bets. It will offend your response of probability. Fiftly, I noticed the extent to which I was heuristic information (rules of thumb) as well as cognitive and emotional biases to form my opinion which I would then act on. For instance a friend of mine mentioned that one of teams was doing really and had great players this year - I trust the friend so I seemed to also trust his judgment on the team. I processed his comments like picking up a `tip' at the racetrack. I also have an emotional bias in favour of this team because I went to school there. Is this really the best I can do in making a decision to `bet' (change the word to `invest') in a positive outcome? Finally, I also began to see the extent to which `heuristics and biases' were apparent in other decisions - such as in my approach to investing and to making decisions generally. This is the real learning for me.<br /><br />Much as I like the book however and much as I have learned from it, there are some things that I think Taleb misses. For instance in his critique of Warren Buffett I think he is exposing himself to being \\"hoisted on his own petard\\". For instance, I am not sure why he takes a cut at Warren Buffett while at the same time demonstrating his admiration for George Soros. Taleb seems to be letting his `irritation' with Buffett get in the way of seeing someone who is truly skilled in probability and risk taking and someone who could be an exemplar for the kind probabilistic thinking he very much favours. Charlie Munger would be another one. Looking through the lens of Taleb's latest book Antifragile, Buffett and Munger have possibly created the most Antifragile business (Berkshire Hathaway) in the S&P 500. Further, Buffett is not at all Fooled by Randomness. If anyone that gets the importance of randomness in life and business it is Buffett. At the core of Buffett's and Berkshire's business is insurance. Buffett's ability to estimate the probability of events and to charge an appropriate risk premium has meant that Berkshire has had a positive combined ratio (premium to payout) for over a 40 year period. Taleb later said (Taleb 2010) that we don't have enough information to say with certainty that Buffett is not a random event. In a very narrow technical sense he may be correct, but in terms of the second-order effects of such a conclusion it is nonsense. Even the most recent academic coverage of Buffett is coming around to the seeing the `Buffett alpha' (Frazini, Kabiller & Pedersen, 2012). Time manages to smooth out much of the impact of `unusual' randomness and in Buffett's case what is `unusual' is that he doesn't outperform the market every year. Over the past 55 years there has only been 8 years in which Buffett has failed to outperform the market. In other words the randomness is in Buffett's failures, not his successes i.e., Buffett's deviation from the `norm' of his own outperformance `can' be explained by luck and randomness and not by any lack of skill. I have been working through these issues in a separate paper entitled, Is Warren Buffett a statistical anomaly? (Kelly, 2006). For instance, when Buffett started out as a funds manager in 1957 there were only 453 listed fund managers (Burton, 1968). The chances of anyone of these achieving the same kind of results as Buffett, by chance, are incredible remote (i.e., 0.0000050%). Perhaps I am however missing something about the underlying randomness of the environment that Buffett operates in that would allow Taleb and others to effectively dismiss him as chance occurring. Finally though, what I think Taleb misses is less important for me than what I think I miss and in that respect I am grateful to him for highlighting the inductive problem in thinking, the importance of qualitative assumptions in bets on probable and indeed improbable outcomes and how emotionally susceptible I am to being Fooled by Randomness.<br /><br />Edward Kelly
