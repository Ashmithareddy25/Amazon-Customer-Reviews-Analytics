An announcement from ESPN in July, 2013 indicated that Silver has taken the role of editor-in-chief of www.FiveThirtyEight.com , a service newly acquired by ESPN.<br /><br /> In The signal and the noise Silver treats the task of predicting the future in the contexts of politics (Chapter 2), the career performance of new players in professional baseball (Chapter 3), weather forecasting (Chapter 4), earthquake prediction (Chapter 5), economic predictions (Chapter 6), the spread of infectious disease (Chapter 7), chess playing (Chapter 9), poker playing (Chapter 10), the stock market (Chapter 11), climate (Chapter 12), and terrorist attacks (Chapter 13).  He uses Chapter 1 to introduce his book, Chapter 8 to introduce the reader to Reverend Thomas Bayes, 1702-1761, whose probabilistic way of thinking about predictions is at the heart of what Silver seeks to teach to forecasters.  Silver uses his concluding chapter to encapsulate his message.<br /><br /> Along the way, Silver seeks to teach some important lessons about forecasting.  One is \\"think probabilistically\\" (p 61).  Another is over-fitting one's statistical model to noisy data is a frequent mistake in the forecasting business and only reduces the accuracy of the forecast (p 163).  Making and testing predictions in order to learn about one's errors and how to improve one's forecasts (mentioned several places including p 452) is a good idea.  Beginning with many hypotheses (reaching for the \\"unknown unknowns,\\" p 420) is a good idea.  These notions appeal to this reviewer as good advice.<br /><br /> This behavioral and management scientist learned to check his forecasting methods against actual outcomes while in training in the 1950s.  It was well known at that time that \\"over-fitting\\" one's statistical model to noisy data produces a model which, when the model is tried for its forecasting accuracy on new data, will cause predictions to shrink significantly below first findings in forecasting accuracy.  Thus repeating a forecasting method at least once using new data was expected for best-practice-in-science, applied or theoretical ... these precautions being in hand half a century ago.  Bradley Efron of Stanford (2013) introduces us to the history of approval, then disapproval, approval again, then more disapproval of Reverend Bayes' thought processes within the fraternity of statisticians in the two and a half centuries since Bayes introduced his method for refining our \\"prior\\" (guessed) probabilities with respect to the likelihood of an event.  Efron acquired his knowledge of Bayes' methods through teaching those methods to university students (2010) and, in his thought processes and advice to researchers, is more \\"frequentist (Fischerian)\\" than \\"probabilist (Bayesian)\\" ... as are most statisticians.  Kahneman (2011) illustrates the flaws and possible corrections for universal human thought processes.  Patterson (2010), Fox (2009), and Taleb (2004) have warned us that stock markets can be random and that statistical models - inadequately thought through and tested - can lead investors into disastrous decisions.  Blinder (2013) competently reviews the multiple contributions that produced the 2007-2009 collapse of the US economy, no small part of that outcome following from all-but-deliberate institutional blunders in understanding the probabilities for unwanted events.  Schmidt and Cohen (2013), building on Google's experience and experience in forecasting events influencing international relations, are well aware of the problems associated with \\"big data,\\" more data than can be absorbed.  Tetlock (2005), whose work is frequently cited by Silver as Sliver writes The signal and the noise, described the nonsense that political forecasting can be.  Valiant (2013) would have us believe that mathematical algorithms can absorb \\"new data\\" and lead us toward \\"probably approximately correct\\" insight into relationships tying what we see now to likely future outcomes ... Valiant, a computer technologist, reporting his work with no knowledge base from psychology, a discipline that has studied how humans learn for the past century and a half and learned a good bit about when people learn, when people don't learn, and when people forget.<br /><br />Does Silver integrate and conceptualize this variety of knowledge so that future forecasts in many fields can be better made?  No.  Does he teach the reader how to distinguish forecasts that can be trusted from those that should be dismissed?  Not particularly.  Silver does understand correctly that every forecast, no matter the field or the method, does need to be reported showing a range of likely outcomes and the statistical probability that the actual outcome will fall outside the reported range ... noting correctly that news media and even scientific journals frequently overlook this essential requirement.<br /><br /> Working for The New York Times, traveling from prediction center to prediction center as a journalist does while gathering material for this book, one expects Silver to write well.  He certainly tells some interesting stories.  But writing fine sentences and paragraphs is not his first strength ... and his editors seem to have given him too little help.<br /><br /> Silver's stories all originate in prediction efforts based in US practice and experience.  There is a larger world out there.<br /><br /> As the list of references here shows, there are more than a few recent tales about how forecasts fail.  We can see the recent successes that Silver has experienced in political forecasts ... and that recommends him to the reader as an adviser about how to identify a trustworthy forecast.  His advice, if you can sift it from his presentation, is middling.  His stories illustrate nicely some of challenges in forecasting.  No mathematical background and no probabilistic fundamentals are needed to read and enjoy Silver's book.<br /><br /> The missing ingredients in this work by Silver are (a) illustrations about how valid forecasts can improve decision-making (I was describing those probabilities to management audiences in 1960 ... and having absolutely no effect on the way decisions were being made ... the audience being totally unprepared to understand and use the information ... and this at Boards of Directors levels in world-class, globally-oriented corporations), (b) integration of the forecasting literature for the general reader (someone needs to do this ... Silver hasn't done it here), and (c) understanding and describing persuasively the immense challenge presented by a general population and a continuing stream of leaders who have learned nothing of probabilistic thinking and statistics in elementary, secondary, or university education.  We need that education as a society so that we can \\"change course\\" and learn to use forecasts, already available, for the benefits they can bring to society.  We behavioral and management scientists have long been able to make much more accurate predictions (about human behavior) than Silver has discovered and presented to us.  Our societies spend important sums and much time understanding and using weather predictions, using orbital mechanics to push satellites to landings on the Moon and Mars, understanding and inhibiting the spread of infectious disease.  We are just now introducing computerized medical record keeping accessible to all our health care providers, and to us, although computers have been widely used in business for at least three decades.  Why aren't we using predictions about human life outcomes already technologically in hand?  I have demonstrated how to do it, and have tried unsuccessfully to publish the news, that productivity in science can be increased fourfold or more at no increase in the current cost of doing science ... but none of the ten or so peer reviewed journals of science and general journals I have approached have been willing to publish the findings.  Why?  Because editors do not know enough to know that the findings are feasible and peers are not responsible enough to look at the evidence, evaluate it, and insist with editors that the report be published.  It is not that we humans cannot learn.  It is that we choose not to learn.  I recently have begun to call it the \\"Won't Learn Syndrome.\\"  Silver understands correctly that the-state-of-the-technology with respect to forecasting needs to be articulated for an audience of `leaders and the well educated' since those audiences have much to learn.<br /><br /> It is possible to learn as a culture, as a society ... as Pinker (2012) so helpfully teaches us with a sweeping look at the history of violent behavior by humans.  Pinker presents data-based arguments persuasively showing that humans are becoming less violent.  Few believe him ... Pinker's evidence be damned.  Learning ... making progress in individual and societal performance ... need not take the nearly one hundred centuries it has taken to reduce people's violent behavior.  Humans have invented science and science is teaching us how to learn ... if only we would learn.  A frequentist's prediction that \\"we won't learn\\" is virtually certain to be accurate.  The Bayesian prior probabilities that we won't learn are about 0.9999 plus or minus about 0.0001.
