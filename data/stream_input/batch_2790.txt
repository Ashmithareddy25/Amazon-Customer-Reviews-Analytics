While waiting for the book to arrive, however, I read two critiques that increased my doubts.<br /><br />First the medievalist Charlotte Allen wrote an Op-Ed in the LA Times (August 4, 2013, page A25), in which she accused Aslan, as I had suspected, of cherrypicking the scriptures `as often as any fundamentalist preacher.' Then, the next day, the New York Times published its review, written by Dale Martin, a chaired professor of religious studies at Yale. It's a harsh review, again accusing Aslan of being capriciously skeptical about some parts of the scriptures, and too credulous about others. At the end, Prof. Martin actually spanked Aslan for being a professor of creative writing rather than a true scholar of ancient Judaism and Christianity. For one academic to publicly put a colleague in his place this way is unusual and, as I said, pretty harsh.<br /><br />So by the time the book arrived I was almost feeling sympathy for Reza Aslan. Then I read the book for myself, and quickly began to feel that Dale Martin was too soft. What Reza Aslan has produced is a novelization of the life of Jesus structured as a historical analysis. It isn't even all that well written, once you take into account how repetitive it is. Some of the repetition is so direct, and so unremarked upon by the author, that it seems like a simple editing oversight.<br /><br />More importantly, the book is dishonest in the sense that it is written so as to seem more original than it really is. Dale Martin noted that Aslan should have noted more clearly the similarity of his thesis (that Jesus was not a pacifist interested only in the next life but rather a conventional Jewish revolutionary with earthly, political goals) to those of earlier authors, specifically S.G.F. Brandon, author of `Jesus and the Zealots.' I would add another book: Hyam Maccoby's `The Mythmaker.' Maccoby reasoned that because there were so many different factions of Judaism in Jesus' time, that the religious arguments Jesus has in the Gospels with other Jews (about divorce law, dietary law, etc.) would not have been out of the ordinary. Therefore, Maccoby argued, it's unlikely that these controversies would have been enough to spur the Jews to toss Jesus at Pilate's feet. It's more likely, Maccoby said, that Jesus was a political rebel against the Roman occupation and was therefore a threat to the high priest of the temple (the high priest being in essence a servant of Rome, a quisling, an insincere Jew, a traitor to the Jewish nation). This is EXACTLY what Aslan claims, yet Aslan never once notes the existence of Maccoby's book, even in his bibliography.<br /><br />To close I'll give two important examples of Aslan's cherrypicking, just to show how profound it is. First, there's the crucifixion. Nothing is more important to Aslan than the fact that Jesus was crucified. Aslan asserts that Jesus' crucifixion tells us that he definitely was a political rebel against Rome, because that's the only crime Rome crucified people for. Now imagine taking a walk in the Jim Crow south, and looking up to see the charred remains of a hanged black man in a tree. Would you conclude that he must have been a murdererbecause that is the only crime that was supposed to be punished that way? No - you would understand that miscarriages of justice were common in that time and place, and that this man might have committed a lesser crime - just about any crime, or perhaps no crime at all. The same, obviously, would be true of a crucifixion victim in an ancient Roman province. Aslan even notes himself - TWICE- on both page 47 and 149, that Pontius Pilate hated Jews and would `eagerly' and `absentmindedly' crucify them without the slightest kind of a trial. In other words, while Jesus may well have been `in fact' a political rebel of the kind that Brandon, and Maccoby, and others, and now Aslan, claim, the bare fact of his crucifixion does not by itself allow us to conclude that as Aslan directly claims.<br /><br />Finally, I have to note Aslan's abuse of Matthew 10:34, which he quotes both in the text (p 120) and on the book's first page. This is the verse in which Jesus says he has not come to bring peace but a sword. Christians have latched on to this verse for centuries, because it's one of the few verses which, if read out of context this way, can be usefully misinterpreted to suggest that the scriptural Jesus wasn't entirely a pacifist - that he understood that sometimes a physical fight is necessary. In fact, whatever and whoever the `historical Jesus' was (and again, no one knows), the SCRIPTURAL Jesus IS entirely pacifist. If you invest just a few seconds into reading Mt 10:32-39, to get a little context (better yet, read the whole chapter, the whole book, or the whole New Testament), Jesus appears to be talking about a metaphorical sword that he will use to split up nuclear families, picking out the believers for salvation and leaving the rest. He is demanding (as he does elsewhere) that his followers think of him and their fellow believers as their true family, whether they are blood relatives or not. He says that believers may find their own blood relatives to be their enemies (may find them to be nonbelievers). Of course Jesus told us to love our enemies so he's not apparently talking about war here - in other words, again, he's apparently not talking about actual, physical swords.<br /><br />But Aslan indicates (without looking at the verses surrounding 10:34, just quoting 10:34 in isolation) that Jesus IS talking about physical, metal swords. He says on p 120 that Jesus `was certainly no pacifist.' Certainly? Jesus wasn't certainly anything. We can say what he was scripturally, and we can surmise that the real Jesus was different from the scriptural Jesus, but no one knows anything `certainly.' He might have been pacifist. He might have been military-political. He might, alternatively, have been a little of each. He might have vacillated, changed his tune depending on the audience and his sense of physical risk. He might have been a bit mad. Aslan says on page 122 that when Jesus said `love your enemies' he was only referring to fellow Jews, not outsiders. But Aslan not only does not know what Jesus meant when he uttered this famous statement - he doesn't even know, no one knows, whether Jesus said it at all.<br /><br />I still would recommend Aslan's `No god but God,' but `Zealot' is a creative work presented irresponsibly, almost laughably, as history.
