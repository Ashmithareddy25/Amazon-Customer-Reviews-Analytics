She was very rash and impulsive in some of her actions, and when confronted with them, she's stumble out something along the lines of \\"I'm just so confused\\" or \\"I don't know why I did that\\" and it just felt weak to me. I was expecting the brave and open girl from The Selection, and I felt like she checked out for most of this book. She did still have some brave moments, but I feel like overall she was less decisive and confident in who she is. Her inability to make (and stick to) a decision kinda drove me crazy.<br /><br />One of the other differences that I didn't like were the actions of Aspen and Maxon. While their characters don't exactly `change' from the first book, it feels like they both said and did things that would make the reader alternatively love, and then hate, them. In one chapter, Maxon would bare his heart and make America (and the reader) love him the most, and then would essentially ignore her for a week. Then in another chapter, Aspen would be in the right place at the right time and make America love him more. This is to be expected in any book with a love triangle, but it happened way too much in this one. I lost count of how many times America \\"made up\\" her mind, and then changed it. One to two times is fine, more than that just makes it feel like drama for the sake of drama.<br /><br />The last thing I'll mention that I disliked in the book is the ending. Without spoilers, the ending is pretty similar to that in The Selection, in that it's fairly abrupt and includes America thinking about the future. In my review for The Selection, I talked about how the abrupt ending didn't bother me as much as many other readers, but it did irk me in this one, likely because I didn't love the whole book like I did the last. I don't like that I waited so long for something that I wasn't crazy about, and I don't like knowing that it'll probably be a year before the conclusion for the series comes out.<br /><br />Now that I've ranted about all the negatives, I will say that I still overall liked the book. There are interesting developments with some of the characters, and I feel like this one tackled the whole \\"dystopian\\" aspect of their world more than the other. Despite America driving me crazy, the ending of this book gave me hope that she'll be stronger in the next installment. The book was still good enough to keep me interested (I read it all in one sitting), and I'm still a fan of the series; I'll just try to lower my expectations for the next book.

Herrington can imitate just about any of the great Dan guitarists, including Becker, although his impressions of Rick Derringer, first heard and seen by this writer on the Two Against Nature House party video CD version of &#34;Black Friday,&#34; are a real retro treat for diehards like me who subscribe to the &#34;Pretzel Logic/Katy Lied- were-the-Dan's-best-work&#34; school. The Presence of McCracken, too, gives this album a bit of the sound of the Royal Scam era.   Fagen continues to employ his trademark approach to electric pianos, using the Fender sound when going for the Jazz rock feel and the Wurlitzer for his send up on Ray Charles. Too, Fagen is one of the best in the biz at using the cheesy organ riff to slide into a catchy, rhythmic pop daydream.  As usual new musicians of incredible talent that no one has heard of appear and reap havoc (principally new guitarists Vignola and Krantz, but some new horn guys as well). Also, Fagen has decided to employ at length something he only synthesized in the past, a harmonica, including numerous solos--new ground for the Dan (except of course the schizoid harmonica on &#34;Show Biz Kids,&#34; which doesn't count).  Finally, under these general comments, is the structural flow of the album.  Fagen uses a reprise of the &#34;Morph the cat&#34; theme which is also the first cut. In doing this Fagen has essentially created an endless circle of music since as the reprise fades away with its great bass line it then jumps back in just as you begin to miss it as cut 1 of the album.  This causes the line to subliminally insert itself into your brain--bump budda-dump dump.  You are ready to go again, and again,.....reminds me of Jethro Tull's bizarre echo line in the middle of &#34;Thick as a Brick&#34;(which only worked on the old 8-track, the album had to be turned over at its most climatic moment, the problem was resolved by the CD).  songs- &#34;Morph the Cat&#34;--heavy density pop meets layered jazz sound. Topic is different...is this like a benign version of M. Night Shyamalan's &#34;Signs&#34;?  Fagen's intro lyrics comments are some help, but a bit of a throw back to &#34;Kamakiriad&#34; with a nod to Fagen's spiritual home of Manhattan. Herrington's guitar solo resurrects the blistering sort of work that Skunk Baxter, Larry Carlton and Rick Derringer used to deliver--a very welcome development...more later.  The vocal harmonies are so rich and dense one is very much reminded of Nightfly's &#34;(Try to Hang on) Maxine.&#34;  &#34;H-Gang,&#34; - another song that once you hear it you program it into your brain's muzak function--a bit like the  &#34;Heard it on the Grapevine&#34; riff but with a sound that reminded me vaguely of a &#34;Royal Scan&#34; track with Herrington doing a &#34;Haitian Divorce&#34;/Larry Carlton guitar number. Harmonica is a very nice touch--Stevie Wonder meets &#34;What I say&#34;.  In the end the song turns into Fagen musing on his own efforts to find himself as &#34;its what I do&#34;. Not sure he has resolved his issues with the topic, though.  Drug reference near the end--the old OD theme.  &#34;Pagoda of Funn.&#34;  Lovers retreat from reality, a ballad about denial.  But Fagen keeps it real with references to Al Jazeera and the looming apocalypse just around the corner.  Too, Fagen's proposal that the institution of marriage is an unlikely (and unrealistic) hiding place from the troubles of the world is on target.  Marriage may just be a bigger challenge than dying stars and severed heads.  &#34;Security Joan.&#34;  Fagen takes the post-911 ordinary and turns it into a romantic song, very upbeat rhythm work by the guitars is reminiscent of &#34;Lunch with Gina.&#34;  Search me now.  &#34;The Night belongs to Mona&#34; - okay, here she is, the seminal Dan-neurotic female.  Again the comments say it all. Too, instead of any outright male losers on this album we have instead this song --the only clear allusion to that theme, with Mona in the title role holding down two themes and &#34;trying to keep it together.&#34; The musical dnouement is on a classic Dan note, the suspended minor ninth chord that trails into the ionosphere (see end of &#34;Gaucho&#34;).  &#34;Mary close the Garden Door&#34;- this song has received a great deal of attention in the blog-o-sphere for its political tone, the worries over the capture of the governmentby, in Fagen's words, &#34;a thuggish cult.&#34;  Stick it to the Bushies, Don!
