The bass, from what I could tell, was mixed properly, but the problem is, like usual on Dream Theater's live recordings, that the bass is slightly too low compared to the rest of the mix (like by 3 decibels). Oddly enough, the level was perfect in Caught in a Web and only a decibel too quiet on the solo on Metropolis, but otherwise it was too quiet to my ears. The keyboards sounded great as did the keytar and Jordan's other instruments. The main issue here is that sometimes his level was perfect while other times he was too low (about the same amount as Petrucci's vocals were too low, like a decibel or two). A tad inconsistent in these instruments, which is why I give them a 3.5/5.<br /><br />As a drummer, the drums are what I know the most about out of these instruments, so this part will be more detailed (sorry, lol). On the whole the drums sound pretty good. This is quite refreshing after how horrible they sounded on the Score DVD, and so I'm fairly pleased. The toms sing, the floor toms rumble, the snares have a great crisp mid-range pop (high range in the case of the secondary snare), and you can really feel the kick drums (3 of them at least). The cymbals are a bit of a mixed bag. First of all, I thought two of his hi-hats and at least one of his stacks were a hair loud. The rest of the cymbals are mixed and leveled about right. The main issue I have with the cymbals here is the panning. Two of the high hats as well as the side splashes and stacks are panned too much for me. Yes, it keeps the drum mix clean and it helps spread everything out, but too much panning is distracting to me. For some reason this sounded a bit better on the 5.1 sound setting than the stereo, but only slightly. Not quite enough. Sometimes panning too much makes things sound like they are a little off even though they're not, so I wish they had not panned those cymbals as much as they did. As a final note on the cymbals, I wish they had a separate mic for the gong. Even though he only uses it once or twice, it was noticeably too quiet and \\"distant\\" sounding. The next issue I had is that the 26\\" bass drum sounded a little weak to me. Yes, I know this is gonna sound too picky, but I think that since it's the biggest kick drum, it should sound the biggest and bassiest. It didn't. It sounds like he doesn't have any dampening in it, and so it has a predominant mid-range attack to it as opposed to the more focused low end punch like the others do. This is quite similar to the problem I had with Mike Portnoy's Albino Monster...the 26\\" kick was disappointingly weak. This is just my opinion, but I think a kick drum that big should blow people back against the wall when you hit it. Instead, it sounded like it was a step down from the rest of them in terms of its power. Speaking of bass drums, the other issue I had was that the Gong Drum sounded dead. I've heard that thing shake the whole arena before, and although I think that there shouldn't be too much low end to it, it could have had quite a bit more. I wanna really feel it through my headphones. Instead it sounded like Mangini was playing on a single headed tom with like 3 felt strips. He does an impressively fast roll on it at the end of Outcry, and I would've liked to get a lot of rumble out of it. Instead, it's a major step down from the largest floor tom (kinda like how the 26\\" kick related to the other ones). Lastly, as crisp and clean as the drums sounded, they were too dry for a live mix. The live drum mix on Live at Budokan had the perfect amount of ambient room sound on the drums, and I feel that same amount should have been present here. The level of the drums was pretty good overall, especially in comparison with the rest of the mix. Drums get a 4/5 from me here.<br /><br />Whew...that was a lot. Those are the things I liked about the mix and things I would change as far as individual instruments. I felt that the audience noise could have been a hair louder, and I feel that the whole mix (especially the drums, as already stated), could have had a little more room sound to it. It sounds sorta like a studio recording with some audience noise in the background. Again, Live at Budokan had the perfect amount of room sound on the instruments, so I wish they would have given more of that to the mix. As a last note, I liked the mix on the CD better than on the DVD. Everything seemed much more well balanced (notperfect, though). In my opinion, the stereo setting on the DVD sounded very plain and quite weak. The 5.1 setting sounds pretty good, so I'd say go with that one. My computer didn't support the surround sound setting, so I couldn't try that one out. Lastly, I think that along with turning the bass up and the guitar down a tiny bit, I would have done a tiny hint of panning on the keys and guitar to make more room for the bass. Not as much as Live at Budokan, though, which despite having good sound had too much panning for my tastes on those instruments. I give the mix a 4/5 here. Not perfect, but still pretty good.<br /><br />Well, I think I covered it all as much as I can. Dream Theater continues to put out great music and live performances, and I think they've still got quite a future ahead of them. Let's all hope and pray they don't go through any more lineup changes anytime soon and that LaBrie gets his hi-mid range to sound like the rest of it, and everything will be awesome! In short, I'd recommend this DVD. I enjoyed it, and I look forward to listening to the music many more times to come. Thanks for reading!
