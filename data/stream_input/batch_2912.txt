This chapter details how combat works, what each person can do on their move, what is allowed, what actions can be performed, and special combat maneuvers.  Pathfinder simplified the combat maneuvers from the previous addition.  Each character has a combat maneuver bonus (CMB) and a combat maneuver defense (CMD), both of which are static numbers that increase as the player levels up.  A player decides on a combat maneuver to perform (such as a bull rush) and in order to succeed, they roll and add their CMB and compare it to the enemy's CMD.  If it's higher, the maneuver succeeds, otherwise it fails and the action is wasted.<br /><br />Chapter 9: Magic<br />This chapter explains how to cast spells, what happens during spell casting or when a character is interrupted, and learning new spells to cast.<br /><br />Chapter 10: Spells<br />This lengthy chapter has a short reference list of each spell at each level for each class. Any spellcaster will want to bookmark this section!<br /><br />Chapter 11: Prestige Classes<br />After fulfilling certain requirements, a character may be eligible to take levels in a prestige class; a specialized class that offers unique and powerful abilities.  This is essentially multiclassing.  This chapter describes each prestige class just as the Chapter 3 did for the core classes.  Classes include the Assassin, Arcane Trickster, Duelist, and Mystic Theurge to name a few.<br /><br />Chapter 12: Gamemastering<br />This chapter gives advice on how to run, prepare, and setup a Pathfinder game.  Included are how to award experience points, build encounters, and distributing treasures.<br /><br />Chapter 13: Environment<br />As it sounds, this chapter deals with the various environment the player may encounter in the game, which may be just as deadly as any enemy they encounter.  This includes, deserts, dungeons, forests, mountains, urban/cities and several more, as well as the various weather players may encounter.<br /><br />Chapter 14: Creating NPCs (Non-Player Characters)<br />Whether a game master needs a villain to terrorize the players, an associate to help them in a pinch, or just an inn keeper to take their gold, this chapter explains all that is needed to create NPCs including simple classes for NPCs, creating statistics quickly, outfitting them, and determining how much of a challenge they may pose to the players.<br /><br />Chapter 15: Magic Items<br />This chapter details the wondrous items the players will ultimately desire to find and use in their adventures.  Basic information regarding magic items, a random generation table, the various abilities that can be enchanted or found on magic items, magic armors, weapons, staffs, rods, wands, rings and items.  There is likely something for everyone in here, and if there isn't, this section also details how to create magic items so players can craft their own fantastical gear.<br /><br />The rest of the book is the appendices for quick reference.  It is well organized and extremely useful.<br /><br />In order to start playing, only two other items are necessary: a set of dice and a copy of the bestiary.  This is not a solo game, however, and two people are needed to play.<br /><br />If you're new to tabletop RPGs, or looking for a new home, this is a great, well developed and supported product to pick.  If you're a fan of D&D 3rd edition (or 3.5) you will feel at home with the rules.  Even better, all your old supplements can easily be used with Pathfinder.<br /><br />This product is a 5/5 for me.  The love and care that Paizo took in creating Pathfinder is obvious, not only with the quality of this book, but with the way they interact with their community.  The character creation is versatile and limitless; each class can be customized for a unique playstyle.  The rules are streamlined and simple(though sometimes questions do come up on how to solve some complex interaction).  But most of all, the game I loved is back and better than ever.  I highly recommend Pathfinder to anyone looking for a great tabletop experience.
