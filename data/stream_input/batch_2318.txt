Yes there are some who are \\"indifferent\\" to the second part of Ummagumma, but I will bet they have a liking of at least some parts.  Non-Floydians might as well pass on this one.  There are reviews aplenty on this set, if you want detailed analysis, but a note for novice Floyd fans.  If you indeed love Pink Floyd, don't be put off by lower review standards.  Some say that there are few tracks on this album to which you will return regularly, that much of it is so experimental that you might listen once and forget it for a few years.  The plain fact is that unless you are one of those (and only IF) who likes DSOTM and Wish You Were Here only, in other words a \\"casual fan\\", and are otherwise indifferent to Pink Floyd, if in fact you are a true Floydian, then I disagree wholeheartedly.  There are tracks by Barrett, incessant and dissonant noodlings, and cacophonies by Waters (Corporal Clegg for example, which worked for me in '68 as a Lennon-styled psychedelic indulgence but has not stood the test of time) which more offend my earsthan the experiments conducted on Ummagumma.  Because of that, and because of the outstanding engineering and mastering done here, I give Ummagumma 5 stars both for the remastering and for the original material in comparison to other masters and other recordings in the same vein.  For Floydians, this album is a treasure, for critical Floydians it is a 4-star effort compared to their masterpieces, for Non-Floydians it is a two star or at best 3 star experiment.  Richard Wright provides a four part keyboard movement suite called Sysyphus, based upon the Greek myth of same-named anti-hero who was punished for his chronic criminal behavior by being forever made to repeatedly push a large boulder up a hill and never attain the top crest.  The suite movement follows a theme which mutates into free-form piano, then into a journey into hades, a breaking dawn on the beautiful shores of the river Styx, punishment, and then then again the theme motif (fashioned after a repeating mellotron motif).  Roger Waters' contribution is a lovely acoustic guitar and poetic vocal track Grantchester Meadows, complete with swimming and splashing effects, birds and minstrels.  Waters double-tracks his vocals nicely to affect a duet and the birds supply an actual \\"other\\" instrument.  G Meadows, by way of a fly-swatter, segues directly into Several Species Of Small Furry Animals Gathered Together In A Cave And Grooving With A Pict.  Often a Halloween favorite to blare on trick-or-treaters, the gist of the track is that one can create animal sounds in the studio using voice and tape effects, hands clapping and leg-slapping, and the end result is quite a feat of engineering.  Actually humorous and enjoyable, some folk might relegate this one to just so much \\"noise\\".  If you still have your old record and an older player, use your hand to slow down the turntable to approximately half the 33-1/3 RPM speed at about four and a half minutes in, and you will hear Roger saying \\"That was pretty avant-garde, wasn't it?\\"  At the end, the Pict is actually speaking old English with a heavily Gaelic accent, but if you pay attention, you will be able to figure out what he is saying without a translator (it is all in the pronunciation, and bear in mind that a \\"Claymore\\" is a sword).  David Gilmore provides the best effort on LP2/CD2.  Although he maintains it was something a little more than rubbish, many fans simply love The Narrow Way.  All sounds made with guitars, it is both a cosmic voyage a la 2001, and an intrepid trilogy of movements from acoustic space to dark matter and on to lyrical poetry of an Odysseus nature.  Mr. Nick Mason finishes off the picture book with a three part suite of his own.  The Grand Vizier's Garden Party is circus of drumming that provides some excellent entertainment and visual imagery for the imaginative (that is).  It opens and closes with Mrs. Mason's flute, the only non-Pink Floyd instrument provided on the whole album.<br /><br />So what you have here, is a picture book of Pink Floyd, the group which will provide masterpiece soundtracks to our lives, and the individual members as parts of the whole.  Highly enjoyable to Pink Floyd cult members, not so for others, but don't knock it until you have tried it.  Huge kudos to James Guthrie and Doug Sax for the RE-mastering of this classic and all the 2011 re-issues, that alone is worth 4 stars.  Floyd albums have always been on the pricey side (ever see one in a cutout bin??? Well, have you?) so don't expect the price of the doubles (Ummagumma, The Wall) in the Discovery series to fall anytime soon (and then they will become 3rd party merchant expensive pieces).
